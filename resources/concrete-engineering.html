Concrete Technology

Core topics every concrete engineer should know

Sixteen foundational subjects — from cement chemistry to precast systems — written as quick-reference cards. Expand any card for a working summary.

Concrete Technology Basics

›

Concrete is a composite of cement, fine and coarse aggregate, water, and (often) admixtures, engineered for strength, workability, and durability.

Its properties are governed by the paste (cement + water) binding aggregate particles; paste quality controls strength and durability, while aggregate grading controls workability and economy.

Types of Cement Materials

›

OPC (33/43/53 grade), PPC, PSC, and Rapid Hardening Cement each suit different exposure and strength needs.

OPC 53-grade gives the highest early strength; PPC and PSC improve durability in sulphate or marine exposure and reduce heat of hydration in mass concrete.

Aggregates Materials

›

Fine aggregate (sand) and coarse aggregate (crushed stone/gravel) make up roughly 60–75% of concrete volume.

Grading, shape, and surface texture govern workability and the water demand of a mix; well-graded aggregate reduces void content and cement requirement.

Water-Cement Ratio Design

›

The single most important factor governing strength, permeability, and durability of hardened concrete.

A lower w/c ratio increases strength and reduces permeability, but too low a ratio without admixtures harms workability — hence the role of superplasticizers.

Admixtures Materials

›

Chemical (plasticizers, retarders, accelerators) and mineral (fly ash, silica fume, GGBS) admixtures modify fresh or hardened properties.

Superplasticizers allow low w/c ratios at workable slump; silica fume boosts strength and reduces permeability for HPC.

Reinforcement Basics Structural

›

Concrete is strong in compression but weak in tension — steel reinforcement (rebar) carries the tensile and shear loads.

Fe415 and Fe500 grade TMT bars are common in India; cover, spacing, and development length are governed by IS 456.

Curing Methods Practice

›

Water curing, membrane curing, and steam curing maintain moisture and temperature for hydration to continue.

Insufficient curing is one of the top causes of surface cracking and reduced long-term strength; minimum curing period is typically 7–14 days.

Durability of Concrete Performance

›

Resistance to carbonation, chloride attack, sulphate attack, and freeze-thaw cycles over the structure's service life.

Durability is controlled through low permeability (low w/c ratio), adequate cover, and appropriate cement type for the exposure condition.

Self-Compacting Concrete SCC

›

SCC flows and consolidates under its own weight, filling formwork and encasing congested reinforcement without vibration.

Achieved through high superplasticizer dosage, viscosity-modifying agents, and carefully controlled aggregate grading.

High-Performance Concrete HPC

›

Engineered for enhanced strength, durability, and workability beyond conventional concrete, often using silica fume and superplasticizers.

Used in bridges, marine structures, and tall buildings where long service life and reduced maintenance are priorities.

Fiber Reinforced Concrete FRC

›

Steel, glass, synthetic, or natural fibers control crack propagation and improve toughness and impact resistance.

Common in industrial floors, pavements, and shotcrete tunnel linings where crack control matters more than added flexural strength alone.

Ready Mix Concrete RMC

›

Batched at a central plant to precise specification, then transported to site by mixer truck within its workable window.

Ensures consistent quality control versus site-mixed concrete, but requires careful scheduling around transit and discharge time.

Precast Concrete Systems

›

Cast and cured in a controlled factory environment, then transported and erected on site.

Improves quality control and speeds construction, common for beams, panels, culverts, and modular building systems.

Quality Control Site Practice

›

Systematic checks on materials, batching, placing, and testing to ensure specified properties are met on site.

Includes slump checks at delivery, cube sampling frequency per IS 456, and batching plant calibration records.

Quality & Testing

Fresh and hardened concrete testing

The three tests every site engineer runs, from a quick field check to laboratory-grade non-destructive evaluation.

Slump Test

Measures workability of fresh concrete by filling a slump cone in three layers, lifting it, and recording the vertical drop.

Typical slump range25–125mm
IS 1199

Cube Compressive Strength Test

150mm cubes cured for 7, 14, and 28 days are crushed under a compression testing machine to verify characteristic strength.

Strength gain by 28 days100%
IS 516

Non-Destructive Testing (NDT)

Rebound hammer, ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV), and core sampling assess in-place strength without damaging the structure.

UPV quality (good concrete)>4.0 km/s
IS 13311

Interview Questions

What is the difference between M20 and M25 concrete? +

M20 has a characteristic compressive strength of 20 MPa at 28 days, M25 has 25 MPa — the numeral after "M" always denotes design strength in N/mm².

Why is the water-cement ratio critical to durability? +

Excess water creates capillary voids after hydration, increasing permeability and letting in moisture, chlorides, and carbon dioxide that corrode reinforcement.

What causes honeycombing in concrete? +

Inadequate compaction, poor mix design, or congested reinforcement prevent mortar from fully surrounding coarse aggregate, leaving voids at the surface.

What is the purpose of curing compounds? +

They form a membrane on the concrete surface that retains moisture for hydration where continuous water curing is impractical.
