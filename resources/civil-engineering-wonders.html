Marvels of the Modern World

The Burj Khalifa

Dubai, United Arab Emirates

The undisputed king of skyscrapers, the Burj Khalifa is a triumph of structural and wind engineering. To prevent the massive wind forces from knocking the 828-meter structure down, engineers utilized a "buttressed core" design.

This Y-shaped floor plan provides high torsional resistance. Furthermore, the building's profile steps back as it rises, confusing the wind and preventing the formation of organized wind vortices that cause buildings to sway violently.

Height: 828 meters (2,717 ft)
Concrete Used: 330,000 cubic meters
Primary Discipline: Structural Engineering
Completion: 2010

The Millau Viaduct

Aveyron, France

Spanning the deep valley of the River Tarn, the Millau Viaduct holds the record as the tallest bridge in the world. It is a multi-span cable-stayed bridge that touches the valley floor in only seven places, minimizing its environmental footprint.

The engineering challenge was immense due to high winds and seismic activity. The deck was constructed on land and rolled out across the pylons using hydraulic rams—a massive feat of precise mechanical and civil synchronization.

Mast Height: 343 meters (1,125 ft)
Total Length: 2,460 meters
Primary Discipline: Bridge Engineering
Completion: 2004

The Panama Canal System

Isthmus of Panama

A masterpiece of hydraulic and geotechnical engineering, the Panama Canal connects the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. It operates using a system of locks that act as massive water elevators, lifting ships 26 meters above sea level to an artificial lake.

The recent massive expansion project added wider locks and water-saving basins. These basins recycle 60% of the water used per transit, solving a critical environmental and hydrological challenge while accommodating modern "Neopanamax" mega-ships.

Length: 82 Kilometers
Earth Excavated: Over 200 million cubic meters
Primary Discipline: Hydraulic Engineering
Opened: 1914 (Expanded 2016)
