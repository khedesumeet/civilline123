📐 Module I – Basic Building Materials I

1. Aggregate

What are aggregates? They are inert granular materials like sand, gravel, or crushed stone. They make up 70–80% of concrete volume and give it body, strength, and reduce shrinkage.

Classification by Shape

  • Rounded: Natural sea/ river gravel. Good workability, but weak bond. Not for high-strength concrete.
  • Angular: Crushed rock. Higher void content but gives 10–20% more compressive strength due to better bonding.
  • Flaky / Elongated: Thin or long particles. Not suitable for concrete as they reduce workability and strength.

Classification by Size

  • Fine Aggregate: Passes through 4.75 mm sieve. e.g., natural sand.
  • Coarse Aggregate: Retained on 4.75 mm sieve. e.g., gravel, crushed stone.

Important Properties

  • Grading: Particle size distribution. Proper grading gives dense, strong concrete.
  • Fineness Modulus: A number (2.2–3.2) indicating coarseness. Higher value = coarser sand.
  • Flakiness & Elongation Index: Percentage of irregular particles. Should be low.
  • Specific Gravity: Usually 2.6–2.8. Higher means stronger aggregate.
  • Water Absorption: Important to adjust water-cement ratio. If aggregate is dry, it absorbs mixing water.
  • Bulking of Sand: Sand volume increases when moist (up to 40%). Must be considered in volume batching.

Soundness & Alkali-Silica Reaction (ASR)

  • Soundness: Resistance to weathering (freeze-thaw). Tested using sodium or magnesium sulfate.
  • ASR (“Concrete Cancer”): Reaction between alkali in cement and reactive silica in aggregate. Produces a gel that expands and cracks concrete. Can be controlled using fly ash, slag, or silica fume.

2. Bricks & Masonry Blocks

Standard Indian brick size: 190 × 90 × 90 mm (with mortar: 200 × 100 × 100 mm).

Types of Bricks (by Quality)

  • First Class: Well-burnt, uniform shape, metallic sound. Crushing strength ≥ 10 N/mm². Used for permanent structures.
  • Second Class: Slightly irregular, moderate strength (≥ 7 N/mm²). For one-storey buildings.
  • Third Class: Under-burnt, soft, irregular. Weak. Used for temporary work.

Field Tests (Simple checks on site)

  • Shape & Size: Uniform rectangular, sharp edges.
  • Hardness: Scratch with fingernail – no impression should remain.
  • Soundness: Strike two bricks – should give a clear metallic ring.
  • Drop Test: Drop from 1 m height – should not break.

Laboratory Tests

  • Water Absorption: Should not exceed 20% of dry weight.
  • Compressive Strength: Minimum 3.5 N/mm² for common bricks.
  • Efflorescence: White salt deposits on surface. Should be “slight” or “moderate” at most.

3. Lime

Lime (calcium oxide) is obtained by burning limestone. It is used in mortar, plaster, and soil stabilization.

Types

  • Quick Lime (CaO): Unstable, reacts violently with water. Must be slaked before use.
  • Slaked Lime (Ca(OH)₂): Quick lime + water. Safe white powder.
  • Fat Lime (High Calcium): Pure lime, slakes vigorously. Used for whitewashing, plastering.
  • Hydraulic Lime: Contains clay. Sets under water. Used in damp conditions.
Good to know: Lime mortar “self-heals” – if micro-cracks form, free lime dissolves, recrystallizes, and seals them.

4. Cement

Portland cement (OPC) is the most common type. It is made by heating limestone and clay to 1450°C, then grinding with gypsum.

Bogue Compounds (main minerals)

  • C₃S (Tricalcium silicate): Responsible for early strength (first 7 days).
  • C₂S (Dicalcium silicate): Gives long-term strength (after 28 days).
  • C₃A (Tricalcium aluminate): Causes fast setting and high heat generation.
  • C₄AF (Tetracalcium aluminoferrite): Helps in clinker formation, gives colour.

Hydration & Testing

  • Hydration: Cement + water → chemical reaction (exothermic) → hardens. Needs moisture and time (curing).
  • Tests: Fineness (sieve test), Consistency (Vicat apparatus), Setting Time (initial ≥ 30 min, final ≤ 600 min), Compressive Strength (mortar cube test), Soundness (Le Chatelier).

5. Fly Ash

Fine powder from coal combustion. It is a pozzolana – reacts with lime to form cementitious compounds.

  • Used in: Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC) and fly ash bricks.
  • Advantages: Improves workability, reduces heat of hydration, increases durability, and uses industrial waste.
🧱 Module II – Mortar & Concrete

1. Mortar

Mortar is a mixture of binding material (cement/lime) + fine aggregate (sand) + water. It bonds masonry units.

Types

  • Cement Mortar: Cement + sand. Strong, for load-bearing walls.
  • Lime Mortar: Lime + sand. Flexible, good for old buildings.
  • Gauged Mortar: Cement + lime + sand. Combines strength and workability.
  • Mud Mortar: Mud + sawdust/cow dung. Used where cement/lime is unavailable.

Tests on Mortar

  • Consistency: Using penetrometer.
  • Compressive Strength: Cube test.
  • Water Retention: How long mortar stays workable.

2. Concrete

Concrete = cement + fine aggregate + coarse aggregate + water (+ admixtures).

Production Stages

  • Batching: Measuring materials by volume or weight (weight batching is more accurate).
  • Mixing: Hand or machine mixing.
  • Transporting: From mixer to site (must avoid segregation).
  • Placing: Pouring into forms carefully.
  • Compacting: Removing air voids (vibration).
  • Curing: Keeping concrete moist to allow hydration. At least 7 days, ideally 28 days.
  • Finishing: Smoothing the surface.

Properties of Fresh Concrete

  • Workability: Ease of placing and compacting. Measured by slump test.
  • Segregation & Bleeding: Separation of ingredients or water rising to surface – undesirable.

Properties of Hardened Concrete

  • Compressive Strength: Most important property. Cube test at 7 and 28 days.
  • Tensile Strength: Low (~10% of compressive).
  • Modulus of Elasticity: Stiffness of concrete.
  • Durability: Resistance to weathering, chemical attack, abrasion.

Factors Affecting Strength

  • Water-Cement Ratio: Lower w/c → higher strength (but less workability).
  • Quality of materials: Cement, aggregates, water.
  • Curing: Proper moisture and temperature.
  • Compaction: 5% voids can reduce strength by 30%!

Admixtures

  • Accelerators: Speed up setting.
  • Retarders: Slow down setting for hot weather.
  • Plasticizers: Improve workability with less water.
  • Air-entraining agents: Improve freeze-thaw resistance.

3. Special Concretes

  • Lightweight Concrete: Density 1400–1840 kg/m³. Uses lightweight aggregates (expanded clay, pumice). Good for insulation.
  • High-Density Concrete: Density > 3360 kg/m³. Uses heavy aggregates (barite, magnetite). For radiation shielding.
  • Vacuum Concrete: Excess water removed by vacuum after placing. Increases strength and surface durability.
  • Shotcrete: Concrete applied at high velocity. Good for vertical/overhead surfaces, tunnels.
  • SFRC (Steel Fiber Reinforced Concrete): Steel fibers (0.3–2.5% volume) improve tensile/flexural strength and ductility.
  • Polymer Concrete: Uses polymer resin instead of cement. High durability, chemical resistance.
  • Ferrocement: Thin cement mortar reinforced with wire mesh. Lightweight, can be shaped easily.
  • High Performance Concrete (HPC): High strength (> 80 MPa) and durability. Uses low w/c ratio, superplasticizers, silica fume.
  • Self-Compacting Concrete (SCC): Flows under its own weight, no vibration needed. Ideal for congested reinforcement.
🪨 Module III – Basic Building Materials II

1. Building Stones

  • Granite: Hard, durable, high strength. Used for foundations, road metal, flooring.
  • Sandstone: Easy to dress, used for ornamental work.
  • Limestone: Used for cement manufacturing and flooring.
  • Marble: Metamorphic, can be polished. Used for flooring, facing, decorative work.
  • Slate: Splits into thin sheets. Used for roofing and damp-proofing.
Key properties: Crushing strength (min 3.5 N/mm²), hardness (for wear resistance), durability, porosity (low), and specific gravity (2.4–2.8).

2. Wood & Wood Products

  • Heartwood: Dark, dead central part. Strong, durable, resistant to decay.
  • Sapwood: Outer, light, living part. More moisture, prone to decay.

Defects in Timber

  • Natural: Knots, shakes (cracks), burls.
  • Insects: Termites, beetles, marine borers.
  • Fungi: Dry rot, wet rot.
  • Seasoning defects: Warping, splitting, case-hardening.

Seasoning (Drying)

  • Natural: Air seasoning – stacking timber in open air (takes months).
  • Artificial: Kiln seasoning – controlled heat and humidity (takes days).

Wood Products

  • Veneer: Thin slice of wood (0.5–0.85 mm). Used for decorative surfaces.
  • Plywood: Layers of veneer glued with grains at 90°. Strong, stable.
  • Fibreboard: Wood fibres bonded with resin.
  • Particle Board: Wood chips bonded with resin. Cheap, used for furniture.
  • Block Board: Softwood strips sandwiched between veneers. Good for doors.

3. Metals

  • Cast Iron: > 2% carbon. Brittle, good in compression. Used for pipes, machine parts.
  • Wrought Iron: < 0.1% carbon. Ductile, malleable. Used for chains, gates, decorative items.
  • Steel: 0.1–2% carbon. Strong, ductile. Used for construction, cars, tools.
  • Aluminium: Lightweight, corrosion-resistant. Used for doors, windows, foils.
  • Copper: Excellent conductor of heat and electricity. Used for wires, plumbing.

Tests on Steel Rebar

  • Tensile Test: Measures yield and ultimate strength.
  • Bend Test: Checks ductility.
  • Hardness Test: Brinell or Rockwell.
  • Impact Test: Measures toughness (ability to absorb energy).

4. Glass, Gypsum, Plastic, Paint, Bitumen

  • Glass: Made from silica sand. Types: annealed (basic), tempered (stronger, safe), laminated (held together by interlayer). Used for windows, facades.
  • Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O): Soft mineral. Used for plaster, drywall, cement additive.
  • Plastic: Synthetic polymer. Thermoplastics (can be remoulded) and Thermosetting (cannot be remoulded). Used for pipes, insulation, packaging.
  • Paint: Base + pigment + vehicle + drier + thinner. Types: oil paint, enamel, emulsion, cement paint.
  • Bitumen: Black, sticky, waterproof. Used for road construction, roofing. Tests: Penetration (hardness), Ductility (stretch), Softening point.
🏛️ Module IV – Basic Building Construction

1. Foundations

Purpose: Transfer building load to the ground safely, prevent settlement, and provide stability.

Types

  • Shallow Foundation: Depth < width. Used when soil near surface is strong. Examples: spread footing, wall footing, combined footing.
  • Deep Foundation: Depth ≥ width. Used when top soil is weak. Examples: pile, pier, caisson.
  • Pile Foundation: Slender column driven or bored into ground. Transfers load by end bearing (on hard rock) or skin friction (along the sides).
  • Raft Foundation: Large continuous slab under entire building. Used for poor soil or heavy loads.
  • Grillage Foundation: Two or more layers of beams (steel or timber) to distribute heavy loads over a large area.

2. Brick Masonry

Systematic arrangement of bricks with mortar.

Types of Bonds

  • English Bond: Alternate courses of headers and stretchers. Strongest, most common.
  • Flemish Bond: Headers and stretchers alternate in each course. Better appearance, but weaker than English bond.
  • Stretcher Bond: All stretchers. Used for half-brick walls.
  • Header Bond: All headers. For thick walls.
Merits of English Bond: Strong, stable, no need for skilled labour.
Demerits: Less attractive than Flemish bond.

3. Stone Masonry

  • Rubble Masonry: Undressed or roughly dressed stones. Cheaper, for low-height walls.
  • Ashlar Masonry: Dressed stones with fine joints (≈3 mm). Strong, attractive, expensive.

4. Cavity Walls & Arches

  • Cavity Wall: Two leaves (inner & outer) with a gap (4–10 cm). Provides insulation and prevents dampness.
  • Arch: Curved structure spanning an opening. Parts: intrados (inner curve), extrados (outer curve), voussoirs (wedge stones), crown (highest point).

5. Doors & Windows

  • Doors: Battened & ledged, framed & panelled, flush, louvered, sliding, rolling shutter.
  • Windows: Casement (hinged), sliding, double-hung, bay, louvered, fixed.
🎨 Module V – Finishing, Services & Special Constructions

1. Wall Finishes

Plastering

Layer of cement/lime mortar applied to walls. Purpose: Protect from weather, provide smooth surface for painting.

  • Methods: Single coat (interior), two coat (common), three coat (superior work).
  • Defects & solutions: Cracks (cure properly), blistering (mix well), efflorescence (use salt-free materials).

Pointing

Finishing mortar joints in masonry. Types: flush, recessed, struck, tuck, V-grooved.

Distempering & Painting

  • Distemper: Water-based, cheaper, less durable. Used on walls.
  • Painting: Protects and decorates. Steps: surface preparation → priming → undercoat → finishing coat.

2. Vertical Communication

  • Stairs: Steps + landings. Types: straight, quarter-turn, half-turn (doglegged), spiral, helical.
  • Ramps: Sloping surface for wheelchairs, vehicles. Occupies more space.
  • Lifts (Elevators): Electric or hydraulic. For buildings > 4 storeys.
  • Escalators: Powered stairs for moving large crowds. Reversible, speed 30–40 m/min.
Good staircase: Width ≥ 90 cm (residential), 1.5–2 m (public). Rise 160–175 mm, tread 250 mm. Headroom ≥ 2.1 m. (2R + G = 600–700 mm)

3. Damp Proofing

Causes: Rain, groundwater, capillary action. Effects: Efflorescence, paint peeling, timber decay, health issues.

D.P.C. (Damp Proof Course) Treatments

  • Foundation: Provide horizontal and vertical DPC (bitumen, felt).
  • Basement: Continuous waterproof membrane (asphalt tanking).
  • Floors: Hardcore filling + DPC membrane under concrete.
  • Walls: DPC at 15 cm above ground level.
  • Roofs: Lime concrete terracing, mud phuska, or bitumen felt with tiles.

4. Fire-Resistant Construction

Principles: Use non-combustible materials; provide thick sections; protect steel with concrete/plaster.

  • Walls & Columns: Bricks, RCC preferred. Steel must be encased in concrete or fireproof cladding.
  • Floors & Roofs: RCC slab, hollow tile, or jack arch. Avoid combustible flooring.
  • Openings: Use steel doors/windows, fire-resistant glass.
  • Escape routes: Staircases, corridors must remain usable during fire.
